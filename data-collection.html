Overview

The data collection module coordinates how we acquire obituary texts and related metadata from a variety of sources, including digitized newspapers, online archives, and funeral program collections. The goal is to assemble a transparent, reproducible corpus that can support both qualitative and computational analysis.

Data Collection Pipeline

At a high level, our data collection choices move through four linked stages: identifying where obituary content lives, deciding which venues and time periods to include, determining how to access and store available metadata, and finally specifying sampling strategies for each source.

Finding obituaries Scan potential platforms and collections (newspapers, archives, funeral programs, cemeteries) to locate where obituary-like texts are available in digital form.
→
Where? Choose which titles, websites, or collections to include and define the broad time horizon (from 18th-century sources through 2024), balancing coverage with feasibility.
→
How? For each source, determine access methods (APIs, scraping, downloads), what metadata are exposed (dates, locations, bylines, section labels, etc.), and how to store them consistently.
→
Sampling Specify source-specific sampling rules (e.g., full coverage vs. monthly draws, page-level filters, keyword screens) so that our final corpus is traceable and comparable across states and time.

Other Potential Sources Identified

Beyond the sources listed above, we have identified several additional types of materials that may help contextualize or extend our obituary corpus: family tree entries from WikiTree, relevant life-story repositories, death certificates, belated obituaries and events held in memoriam, and historical event records that spur commemorative writing.

We have not yet tapped into these sources systematically or devised detailed methodologies for incorporating them into the project. They are flagged here as future directions for expanding the scope of the data.

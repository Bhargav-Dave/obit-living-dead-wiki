Overview

The data collection module coordinates how we acquire obituary texts and related metadata from a variety of sources, including digitized newspapers, online archives, and funeral program collections. The goal is to assemble a transparent, reproducible corpus that can support both qualitative and computational analysis.

Short version: where our texts come from, what we keep, and how we keep track of them.

Sources & coverage

We are currently focusing on English-language obituaries from Pennsylvania and Georgia, with particular attention to Black newspapers and community funeral programs. Over time, the corpus may expand.

  • Digitized historical newspapers (e.g., state and library portals, ProQuest collections).
  • Community and Black press outlets with obituary sections or death notices.
  • Funeral programs and memorial brochures (scanned PDFs or images with OCR).
  • Supplementary local histories or directories when they help identify individuals or institutions.

Each source brings its own constraints on what can be scraped or downloaded, which are documented here as they arise.

Workflows & tooling

Data collection relies on a mix of manual download, semi-automated scripts, and fully automated scraping, depending on the site and its terms of use.

  • Manual curation and download for small or sensitive collections (e.g., community funeral programs).
  • Scripted collection for large, public newspaper portals, respecting documented usage and rate limits.
  • Use of headless browsers or Selenium for sites that require navigation, login, or dynamic rendering.
  • Consistent directory and file naming conventions to make later processing reproducible.

As scripts stabilize, this page can link out to public-safe documentation and, where appropriate, to code repositories.

Metadata & tracking

Alongside the texts themselves, we actively track metadata needed for both analysis and stewardship.

  • Publication title, location, and known political or community orientation.
  • Issue date, page number, and section (when available).
  • File paths, URLs, and identifiers that allow us to re-trace the origin of each item.
  • Notes on quality issues (e.g., OCR quality, scan artifacts, missing pages).

Internal tracking tables and logs are designed so that we can always answer “Where did this text come from?” and “What have we done to it so far?”

Current decisions

Some working choices in the data collection pipeline include:

  • Erring on the side of collecting a broad set of death-related items initially, with later filtering into stricter obituary categories.
  • Treating institutional constraints (e.g., access limits, download caps) as design constraints to be documented rather than ignored.
  • Logging all automated downloads with timestamps and basic diagnostics (e.g., success, timeout, block).
  • Avoiding scraping from sites that explicitly prohibit automated access, unless separate arrangements are made.

Risks & open questions

Data collection raises ethical, legal, and technical questions that we keep revisiting:

  • How do we handle contemporary obituaries where privacy concerns may be more acute?
  • What constraints do licenses and terms of use place on sharing derived data or models?
  • Where might our collection practices systematically miss communities or publications?
  • How can we design the pipeline so that new sources can be added without breaking earlier steps?

Module overview

Constructing the obituary corpus

The data collection effort assembles obituaries from multiple, partially overlapping sources into a coherent research corpus for Pennsylvania and Georgia from the 18th century to the present.

Because there is no simple “list of all obituaries,” we piece together coverage from:

  • State-sponsored digital archives for historical newspapers.
  • Chronicling America (Library of Congress) for additional titles and earlier years.
  • ProQuest Historical Black Newspapers for Black-focused print outlets.
  • Funeral program collections and Black cemetery repositories.
  • Legacy.com as a key online platform for contemporary obituaries.

Sources

Sources & coverage

For each state, we rely on a small number of key repositories, each with different strengths and gaps.

  • State archives. Georgia Historic Newspapers and the Pennsylvania Newspaper Archive provide digitized pages from hundreds of titles, including many local and regional papers.
  • Chronicling America. Complements the state archives, especially for earlier decades and titles that cross state lines.
  • ProQuest Historical Black Newspapers. Supplies obituaries and death coverage from historically Black newspapers (e.g., Atlanta Daily World, Philadelphia Tribune).
  • Funeral programs and cemetery sites. Collections from the Digital Library of Georgia and Black cemetery projects capture African American funerary practice not always represented in newspapers.
  • Legacy.com. A major online platform for post-2000 obituaries, used to build a large contemporary sample.

Pipeline

Scraping & retrieval pipeline

Each source has its own interface and constraints, so the pipeline is modular. At a high level:

  • Metadata first. For each archive or platform, we compile metadata tables (title, date, URL, basic descriptors) to guide scraping and to track coverage.
  • Automated retrieval. Scripts download page images/text (state archives, Chronicling America) or obituary records (Legacy.com, cemetery/funeral sites), subject to terms of use and rate limits.
  • Obituary detection. For historical pages, classifiers and keyword rules are applied to extract candidate obituary snippets from full pages.
  • Storage & versioning. Outputs are stored in structured formats (e.g., CSV/Parquet) with documented versions so later cleaning and processing are traceable.

Sampling

Sampling strategy across sources

Since we cannot exhaustively download every page ever printed, we aim for a transparent and reproducible sampling strategy that balances coverage and feasibility.

  • Legacy.com. For each year and state (2000–2024), we select two months approximately six months apart and retrieve up to 1,000 obituaries per month. Earlier years often yield fewer records per month, later years more.
  • Historical newspapers. We prioritize titles with long runs and meaningful geographic coverage, then sample issues at regular intervals (e.g., every N years) while also targeting historically important moments.
  • Black newspapers and programs. Because coverage is uneven and often sparse, we oversample from Black-focused outlets (newspapers, funeral programs, cemeteries) rather than treating them as proportional to their print circulation.
  • Celebrities vs. “ordinary” obits. A separate tract identifies Pennsylvania/Georgia-born celebrities via external lists (e.g., Wikipedia) and locates their obituaries and genealogical information for comparison.

A more technical description of the sampling frame lives in the internal “Data collection” methods document. Links will be added from this section once finalized.

Status

Current datasets (high-level)

As of this stage in the project, the following major components are in place:

  • A large Legacy.com sample for Pennsylvania and Georgia (2000–2024), with metadata and raw text for tens of thousands of obituaries.
  • Initial batches of historical obituaries from Georgia and Pennsylvania state archives and Chronicling America, focused on selected titles and time slices.
  • Prototype datasets from Black cemetery and funeral program sites, including shorter, more concise obituaries and program-based narratives.
  • A preliminary “Temporary Obituary Viewer” app (internal) used to inspect and validate early automated extraction from historical pages.

Counts and tables are being updated as scraping and cleaning proceed and will be summarized in internal dashboards rather than on this public page.

In progress

Open questions & decisions ahead

Several data collection issues remain under active discussion:

  • How to measure and report who is missing from the corpus using death certificates, vital statistics, or genealogical data.
  • How to best distinguish between news stories about death and obituaries, especially in early newspapers where the boundary is blurry.
  • How to systematically incorporate interviews and consultations with archivists, funeral home staff, and obituary editors as part of “data collection.”
  • How to document and share the assembled dataset publicly after the period of proprietary access.

These questions are being tracked in SPARC proposal revisions, meeting notes, and emails with partners (e.g., Digital Library of Georgia, CDSAI). Links will be added here once internal documentation is stable.

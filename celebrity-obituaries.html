Overview

The celebrity obituary set provides a complementary perspective to local and regional obituaries. It focuses on nationally visible figures whose deaths are covered across multiple outlets and platforms, including major newspapers, online news sites, and collaborative knowledge projects.

These obituaries tend to be highly curated, with detailed life histories and clearly articulated public narratives. Comparing them to local obituaries helps highlight differences in how social status, achievement, and kinship are represented in public memory.

Sources

The celebrity dataset currently draws on three main types of sources:

  • WikiTree: collaborative family tree entries that often contain obituary text, memorial notes, and detailed kinship structures.
  • Wikipedia lists: curated lists of notable individuals, especially lists of deaths by year or specific thematic aggregations, which help identify celebrities who have Wikipedia pages and documented death dates.
  • National outlets: obituaries published in major newspapers and magazines (e.g., national U.S. outlets) for highly visible figures.

Wikipedia-Based Workflow

A central task is to create structured lists of deceased celebrities and then use those lists to locate associated obituaries and memorials. The rough workflow is:

  • Use Wikipedia lists of deaths (and other relevant lists) to identify people with Wikipedia pages who died within specified year ranges.
  • From these lists, build a consolidated roster of “celebrity” names, with basic attributes such as death date, occupation, and primary geographic ties.
  • Use those names as queries to locate obituaries in national outlets and, where relevant, in Pennsylvania and Georgia–linked newspapers (or other local media).

These steps help us construct separate celebrity lists for Pennsylvania and Georgia, based on either place of birth, residence, or strong life-course connections to those states.

WikiTree & Kinship

WikiTree is used in two ways. First, for particular names on the celebrity lists, we look up their WikiTree profiles to:

  • Locate obituary text or memorial notes embedded in the profile.
  • Extract kinship ties (parents, spouses, children, siblings) that can later be connected to obituary narratives.

Second, more broadly, we use WikiTree as a large-scale genealogical resource in conjunction with the Wikipedia-derived lists to explore how far one can go from a name to a structured representation of relatives and, ultimately, to candidate obituaries.

This broader integration of WikiTree and obituary data is still being clarified and formalized, and the exact procedures may change as the project evolves.

Data Location & Processing

All compiled celebrity lists, intermediate matching files, and scraped obituary texts are stored in the relevant Teams folder. File naming and folder structure are being standardized so that connections to the Pennsylvania and Georgia components are clear.

For more information on how this data is processed and integrated with the broader corpus, visit the Data Processing & Design page.

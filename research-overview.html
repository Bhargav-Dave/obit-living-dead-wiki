Research Overview

This module provides cross-cutting information for the project. The study focuses on how our multi-method approach interprets obituary texts as historical and social documents. The sections below consolidate materials that span methods and workflows: proposals, sites, code, and bibliography.

Sites

Pennsylvania and Georgia were selected for their locations in the Northeast and Southeast, respectively, and for their historically significant black populations. Together they give us two states in distinctive regions with long historical records and rich black press traditions.

  • Regional contrast with long coverage. Georgia represents the South and Pennsylvania the Northeast. Both have state-sponsored newspaper archives with content going back to the eighteenth century (for example, Georgia Historic Newspapers starts in 1763), giving us extended time series for historic obituaries.
  • Downloadable and comparable sources. Both states provide large, relatively accessible sets of digitized newspaper pages and obituaries (through state archives and Chronicling America), which can be sampled in parallel ways.
  • Population scale. Today the two states have broadly comparable population sizes, supporting comparisons in the volume and composition of obituaries over time.
  • Black newspapers and communities. Each state has historically important black communities and black newspapers, allowing us to study how race, region, and local histories shape obituary practices.

See Data collection for state-by-state details (interfaces, coverage years/months, and the total number of obituaries available in each source).

More detailed justification, including maps of newspaper locations, counts of titles, and tables of African American newspapers by state, is documented in team internal materials.

Coding scheme

A draft coding scheme for qualitative work has already been developed. That scheme is our starting point for reading and annotating obituaries, and we will continue to develop and refine codes as we review more texts. We began with qualitative close reading of a diverse set of obituaries to develop this scheme.

The draft coding scheme currently targets (noting where information is known/available versus DK: not known/unavailable):

  • Basic descriptive information (where available) such as age, gender, race, occupation, education, title, locations lived and died, religious affiliation, and (when applicable) celebrity/public status,
  • Kinship and household ties (spouses, in-laws, children, siblings, grandparents, other extended kin, and non-kin such as friends and neighbors), including order in the text and whether individuals are named,
  • References to status, inequality, and community roles (public offices, organizational memberships, religious participation and official roles, military service, and civic contributions),
  • Institutional arrangements for death (e.g., visitation, funeral, receiving/repast, and related descriptors),
  • Narrative features such as how a life story is structured, what kinds of events are highlighted, descriptors of paid and unpaid work, how death is described, personal attributes/interests, and disclosures (both positive and negative), as well as commentary on people listed.

As close reading proceeds, we expect to refine this coding scheme iteratively and record revisions back into the shared project materials. For both qualitative and quantitative analyses, we will compare coded patterns across periods and between states and media, and assess how these patterns vary by available descriptive information.

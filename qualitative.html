Module overview

What the qualitative work is doing

The qualitative strand of The Living Dead treats obituaries as cultural texts rather than neutral records. We read them as narratives that construct collective memory: deciding whose lives are worth narrating, which relationships are visible, and how inequalities are framed or erased.

The core aims are to:

  • Trace how familial, community, and organizational ties are narrated across time.
  • Identify shifting values and cultural narratives (e.g., loneliness, belonging, duty, achievement).
  • Understand how inequalities of public memory appear by gender, race, and class.

Design

Methodology & close reading of texts

Qualitative work proceeds in iterative waves. We begin with small, carefully chosen sets of obituaries (by period, place, race, and gender) and move between detailed reading and emerging codes.

  • Historical sampling. For each state and period, we select focused batches of obituaries (e.g., early 19th century vs. mid-20th century; Black vs. white newspapers; funeral programs vs. daily papers).
  • Context-first reading. Obituaries are read alongside historical notes (e.g., war, migration, legal changes, civil rights struggles) to situate how lives are framed in their time.
  • Comparative reading. We explicitly compare cases (e.g., husband vs. wife obits, Black vs. white obits in the same city and year) to surface patterned differences.

Structure

Creating and refining coding schemes

The coding system is being built to bridge qualitative nuance and quantitative analysis. It captures both who appears in an obituary and how they are described.

  • Core dimensions. Demographic markers (where available), types of ties (spouses, partners, children, extended kin, “chosen family”), organizational membership, work, faith, and community roles.
  • Ordering and emphasis. Who is mentioned first? Who is named vs. left generic (“a host of loving friends”)? How much text is devoted to different spheres of life?
  • Language and framing. Terms of affection, moral judgments, references to health, cause of death (including suicide and violence), and depictions of inequality.

Early coding is exploratory—multiple readers code the same small sets of obituaries, compare interpretations, and adjust definitions before scaling up.

Data choices

Reasoning about data choices & limits

Not every death generates an obituary, and not every obituary survives in accessible archives. The qualitative work continually foregrounds these limits when interpreting findings.

  • Elites vs. “ordinary” lives. Daily newspapers and online platforms overrepresent certain groups (by class, race, and geography). We use subsamples and comparison to vital statistics to keep this visible.
  • Black funeral programs & newspapers. In many places, Black lives were documented through funeral programs or community-specific outlets rather than mainstream dailies. These sources are treated as analytically distinct, not just “add-ons.”
  • Death notices vs. obituaries. Brief death notices without meaningful biographical detail are excluded from the main qualitative coding, but tracked separately so we do not overinterpret silence.

Status

Current status & planned next steps

At this stage, qualitative work has focused on:

  • Reviewing early newspapers to understand when and how obituaries emerge as a distinct genre (versus scattered death notices).
  • Comparing Black and white obituaries and funeral programs to document differences in visibility, length, and narrative focus.
  • Drafting and iteratively revising the coding scheme, especially around social ties, inequality, and the language of loneliness/belonging.

Upcoming qualitative tasks include:

  • Conducting a small pilot comparing two time periods with fully coded subsamples.
  • Preparing case-based vignettes to sit alongside tables and figures in future papers.
  • Exploring interviews or conversations with obituary writers and funeral home staff as a potential supplementary method.

Overview

The qualitative component of The Living Dead focuses on close reading of obituary texts to understand how people and institutions narrate lives, deaths, and relationships across time. This work informs how we define entities, ties, and categories in the quantitative pipeline.

Short version: slow reading first, then careful abstraction into codes and concepts.

Guiding questions

Some of the questions driving the qualitative work include:

  • How do obituaries describe kin, friends, co-workers, neighbors, and organizational ties?
  • Which forms of inequality (race, class, gender, religion, migration status) are made visible or hidden?
  • How do narrative structures and tone change across time, place, and publication type?
  • What counts as an “obituary” versus a death notice, memorial, or news article about a death?

Methods & materials

The qualitative work combines classic interpretive methods with structured note-taking, so that insights can be translated into coding rules and data definitions.

  • Close reading of sampled obituaries from different time periods, locations, and outlets.
  • Analytic memos that track emerging concepts, tensions, and edge cases.
  • Side-by-side comparison of obituaries within families, across racialized groups, and across publications.
  • Coordination with the data processing team to flag patterns that need special handling (e.g., nicknames, titles).

Coding schemes

Qualitative work underpins the coding schemes that later get operationalized as structured variables or patterns for automated detection.

  • Proposed categories for relationship types (e.g., spouse, child, church member, civic role).
  • Indicators of inequality and stratification (e.g., occupational prestige, property ownership, honors).
  • Markers of place and mobility (e.g., birthplaces, migrations, military service, diaspora links).
  • Narrative features (e.g., length, voice, evaluative language) that may correlate with race, class, or newspaper type.

As these schemes stabilize, they will be documented here with examples and links to internal coding manuals.

Current decisions

Some working decisions (subject to revision as the project evolves):

  • Treat “obituaries” broadly at first, then refine into subtypes (formal obituary, brief notice, memorial piece) as we see stable patterns.
  • Anchor relationship coding on explicit textual cues (e.g., “beloved sister of”) before inferring more ambiguous social roles.
  • Use qualitative examples to test and critique automated extraction rules before scaling up.
  • Maintain a shared set of “tricky cases” (PDFs, clippings, borderline texts) as teaching examples for the team.

Open questions

This module is intentionally iterative. Some questions we expect to revisit:

  • When does a mention of someone become a “tie” we want to represent in the network?
  • How do we handle euphemistic or coded language around race, sexuality, or cause of death?
  • What kinds of stories or communities are systematically missing from our sources?
  • How should qualitative findings be archived so they remain useful as the project expands?

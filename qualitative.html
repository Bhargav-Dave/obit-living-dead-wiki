Qualitative Work

The qualitative module focuses on how we interpret obituary texts as historical and social documents. Here we ask what obituaries reveal about kinship, community, inequality, and remembrance, and how those patterns change across time and place.

At this stage many details are still to be finalized. This page records high-level decisions that anchor the qualitative work: why we selected Pennsylvania and Georgia, how our coding scheme is being developed, and how close reading will be coordinated with other parts of the project.

Find the current SPARC proposal grant here.

Why Pennsylvania & Georgia?

Pennsylvania and Georgia were selected for their locations in the Northeast and Southeast, respectively, and for their historically significant Black populations. Together they give us two states in distinctive regions with long historical records and rich Black press traditions.

  • Regional contrast with long coverage. Georgia represents the South and Pennsylvania the Northeast. Both have state-sponsored newspaper archives with content going back to the eighteenth century (for example, Georgia Historic Newspapers starts in 1763), giving us extended time series for historic obituaries.
  • Downloadable and comparable sources. Both states provide large, relatively accessible sets of digitized newspaper pages and obituaries (through state archives and Chronicling America), which can be sampled in parallel ways.
  • Population scale. Today the two states have broadly comparable population sizes, supporting comparisons in the volume and composition of obituaries over time.
  • Black newspapers and communities. Each state has historically important Black communities and Black newspapers, allowing us to study how race, region, and local histories shape obituary practices.

More detailed justification, including maps of newspaper locations, counts of titles, and tables of African American newspapers by state, is documented in the team Google doc and related internal memos.

Coding scheme

A draft coding scheme for qualitative work has already been developed and saved by Eunkyung in the relevant Teams folder. That scheme is our starting point for reading and annotating obituaries.

At a high level, the coding scheme is designed to capture:

  • Basic descriptive information (where available) such as age, gender, race, occupation, and religious affiliation,
  • Kinship and household ties (spouses, children, siblings, extended kin, and non-kin who are described in kin-like ways),
  • References to status, inequality, and community roles (e.g., public offices, organizational memberships, military service, and civic contributions),
  • Narrative features such as how a life story is structured, what kinds of events are highlighted, and how loss and memory are framed.

As we begin close reading, we expect to refine this coding scheme iteratively and record any revisions back into the Teams materials.

Qualitative analysis plan

The core of the qualitative work will be close reading of obituaries sampled across time, place, and source. We are especially interested in how descriptions of kin ties, community ties, and inequality shift from the eighteenth century to the present.

  • We will select sets of obituaries from Pennsylvania and Georgia at different time points and from different kinds of outlets (state archives, Black newspapers, contemporary platforms such as Legacy.com).
  • For each set, readers will annotate obituaries using the shared coding scheme, with attention to kin relationships, language of belonging and exclusion, and how the dead are located in families, neighborhoods, and institutions.
  • We will compare coded patterns across periods and between states, asking how the portrayal of kinship, race, class, and community changes over time and across media.

These qualitative readings will both stand on their own and inform subsequent computational work, including how variables and training examples are defined for information extraction and network construction.

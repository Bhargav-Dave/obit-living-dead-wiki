Research Design

This page records design choices and emerging analysis outcomes as the project develops. It brings together decisions that shape sampling, coding, and interpretation across sources and time periods, and it documents how close reading is coordinated with structured data work.

At this stage, some details are still being finalized. The sections below summarize the project’s core state- and source-selection logic, the coding scheme that anchors both qualitative interpretation and systematic extraction, and the analysis plan that guides comparisons across race, gender, social class, historical period, and format (e.g., print vs. digital; obituary vs. death notice vs. pamphlet).

Why Pennsylvania & Georgia?

Pennsylvania and Georgia were selected for their locations in the Northeast and Southeast, respectively, and for their historically significant Black populations. Together they give us two states in distinctive regions with long historical records and rich Black press traditions.

  • Regional contrast with long coverage. Georgia represents the South and Pennsylvania the Northeast. Both have state-sponsored newspaper archives with content going back to the eighteenth century (for example, Georgia Historic Newspapers starts in 1763), giving us extended time series for historic obituaries.
  • Downloadable and comparable sources. Both states provide large, relatively accessible sets of digitized newspaper pages and obituaries (through state archives and Chronicling America), which can be sampled in parallel ways.
  • Population scale. Today the two states have broadly comparable population sizes, supporting comparisons in the volume and composition of obituaries over time.
  • Black newspapers and communities. Each state has historically important Black communities and Black newspapers, allowing us to study how race, region, and local histories shape obituary practices.

More detailed justification, including maps of newspaper locations, counts of titles, and tables of African American newspapers by state, is documented in the team Google doc and related internal memos. For source-specific coverage and sampling notes, see Data collection and Research overview.

Coding scheme

A draft coding scheme has already been developed and saved by Eunkyung in the relevant Teams folder. That scheme is our starting point for reading and annotating obituaries, and it also anchors what we attempt to extract consistently across sources and time periods.

At a high level, the coding scheme is designed to capture:

  • Basic descriptive information (where available), such as age, gender, race, occupation, education or titles, religious affiliation, locations lived and died, and (when relevant) celebrity status,
  • Kinship and household ties (spouses, in-laws, children, siblings, extended kin, and non-kin described in kin-like ways such as friends and neighbors), including whether individuals are named and how they appear in the text,
  • Status, inequality, and community roles (e.g., public offices, organizational memberships, religious participation and official roles, military service, and civic contributions),
  • Institutional arrangements for death (e.g., visitation, funeral services, receiving lines, and other ritual descriptors),
  • Narrative features such as how a life story is structured, what events are highlighted, paid and unpaid work, how death is described, personal attributes and interests, and other disclosures (both positive and negative), including commentary on people listed.

As we begin close reading, we expect to refine this coding scheme iteratively and record any revisions back into the Teams materials. A streamlined summary of this scheme also appears on the Research overview page.

Qualitative analysis plan

The qualitative component centers on careful, repeated close reading of obituaries sampled across time, place, and source. We are especially interested in how descriptions of kin ties, community roles, inequality, and remembrance shift from the eighteenth century to the present.

  • We will select sets of obituaries from Pennsylvania and Georgia at different time points and from different kinds of outlets (state archives, Black newspapers, contemporary platforms such as Legacy.com).
  • For each set, readers will annotate obituaries using the shared coding scheme, with attention to how the deceased are located in families, institutions, neighborhoods, and broader public life.
  • We will compare coded patterns across periods and between states, asking how the portrayal of kinship, race, social class, and community changes over time and across media.

We anticipate using NVivo to develop analytically grounded nodes with attached attributes, and to run queries that identify patterns and trends in obituary content using procedures commonly applied to open-ended qualitative material.

Mixed-method analysis

After basic cleaning, we focus on extracting information that can be analyzed systematically. This combines close reading, hand annotation, and large language models (LLMs), with cross-checks against human-coded data and comparisons across multiple model outputs.

  • Systematic coding for quantitative analysis. The coding framework (see Research overview) will form the basis for structured datasets used in Stata and R for data management and statistical modeling. We aim to code texts using LLMs, then validate outputs against human-coded material and alternative LLM-coded versions, enabling comparisons across women and men, across race, across generations, and across formats (e.g., print vs. digital; obituary vs. death notice vs. pamphlet).
  • Qualitative interpretation and case development. We expect to rely on the qualitative material for two purposes: (1) to provide stories and quotations that clarify and elaborate the patterns observed in structured data; and (2) to develop case studies that highlight similarities and differences across social groups—particularly between celebrities and ordinary individuals, Black and white communities, and women and men (and, where appropriate, their intersections).
  • Historical comparison and periodization. Using complementary methods, we will trace general changes from the 18th to the 21st century as well as shifts associated with key historical periods (for example, the Civil War, World Wars, the Great Depression, and the passage of the Nineteenth Amendment).
  • Cause-linked and event-linked shifts. We will also examine changes in the availability and character of obituaries linked to particular causes and collective events, such as collective violence, lynchings, suicides, and pandemics including HIV/AIDS and COVID.

Together, these steps support a mixed-method approach to tracing the evolution of obituaries as accounts of who and what is memorialized (and marginalized), and how shifting inequalities shape practices of remembrance.

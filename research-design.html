Research Design

This page records design choices and emerging analysis outcomes as the project develops. It documents how close reading is coordinated with structured data work, and it captures the analysis procedures we use to compare obituary content across time, place, and media.

For cross-cutting project information that is stable and shared across modules—proposals, site selection rationale, and the current coding scheme—see Research overview. This page focuses on how those foundations are implemented in practice: sampling for reading, qualitative workflows, model-assisted coding validation, and planned quantitative and network analysis outputs.

Qualitative analysis plan

The qualitative component centers on careful, repeated close reading of obituaries sampled across time, place, and source. We are especially interested in how descriptions of kin ties, community roles, inequality, and remembrance shift from the eighteenth century to the present.

  • We will select sets of obituaries from Pennsylvania and Georgia at different time points and from different kinds of outlets (state archives, Black newspapers, contemporary platforms such as Legacy.com).
  • For each set, readers will annotate obituaries using the shared coding scheme, with attention to how the deceased are located in families, institutions, neighborhoods, and broader public life.
  • We will compare coded patterns across periods and between states, asking how the portrayal of kinship, race, social class, and community changes over time and across media.

We anticipate using NVivo to develop analytically grounded nodes with attached attributes, and to run queries that identify patterns and trends in obituary content using procedures commonly applied to open-ended qualitative material.

We expect to rely on qualitative material for two purposes: (1) to provide stories and quotations that clarify and elaborate patterns observed in structured datasets; and (2) to develop case studies that highlight similarities and differences across social groups— particularly between celebrities and ordinary individuals, Black and white communities, and women and men (and, where appropriate, their intersections).

Mixed-method analysis

After basic cleaning, we focus on extracting information that can be analyzed systematically. This combines close reading, hand annotation, and large language models (LLMs), with cross-checks against human-coded data and comparisons across multiple model outputs.

  • Systematic coding for quantitative analysis. Using the coding framework (see Research overview), we will build structured datasets and analyze them in Stata and R for data management and statistical modeling. We aim to code texts using LLMs and validate outputs against human-coded data and alternative LLM-coded versions, enabling comparisons across women and men, across race, across generations, and across formats (e.g., print vs. digital; obituary vs. death notice vs. pamphlet).
  • Network analysis outputs. Where the text supports it, we will translate relational information into network-ready formats (e.g., kinship networks, affiliation networks, and place-based ties) and analyze how connectivity, tie composition, and social boundary markers vary across groups and historical periods. (Network analysis specifics will be expanded as we finalize the last-meeting plan and the first extraction pilots.)
  • Historical comparison and periodization. Using complementary methods, we will trace general changes from the 18th to the 21st century as well as shifts associated with key historical periods (for example, the Civil War, World Wars, the Great Depression, and the passage of the Nineteenth Amendment).
  • Cause-linked and event-linked shifts. We will also examine changes in the availability and character of obituaries linked to particular causes and collective events, such as collective violence, lynchings, suicides, and pandemics including HIV/AIDS and COVID.

Together, these steps support a mixed-method approach to tracing the evolution of obituaries as accounts of who and what is memorialized (and marginalized), and how shifting inequalities shape practices of remembrance.
